SaaS, B2B · 2024

Orbit Analytics
Dashboard

Redesigning the core analytics experience for a B2B SaaS platform

The Challenge

The existing dashboard had grown organically over 5 years, resulting in feature bloat and a steep learning curve. Users were churning within the first week due to overwhelming complexity.

My Role

Lead Product Designer — research, IA, interaction design, design system, developer handoff

Outcome

40% increase in daily active usage, 3-point NPS improvement, reduced onboarding time from 4 days to 6 hours

Untangling
complexity

The project began with an extensive research phase — 24 in-depth user interviews across three customer segments. What we heard repeatedly wasn't frustration with missing features, but a sense of paralysis at the sheer number of things on screen at once.

By mapping every interview against a jobs-to-be-done framework, three core use cases emerged that accounted for over 80% of sessions. Everything else was noise. This gave us a clear mandate: design around the jobs users actually hire the product for, and move the rest out of the way.

We also ran a card sorting exercise with 14 participants to understand how they mentally grouped information — the results directly challenged the existing IA and gave us the evidence needed to advocate for a structural overhaul.

Building
clarity

The redesign was anchored on three principles: progressive disclosure, a navigation model that reflected the user's mental model, and a component library that enforced consistency across the product.

Progressive disclosure meant surfacing only the controls relevant to the user's current task. Advanced configuration was accessible, but never visible until needed. We introduced a persistent left-rail navigation with clear hierarchy, replacing the previous tab-soup that had accumulated over years of ad hoc feature additions.

The new component library — built in Figma with comprehensive documentation — became a shared language between design and engineering. Every token was named semantically, every component had a defined set of variants, and every state was accounted for before a single line of code was written.
