Research · Health Economics

Business Models for Telemedicine

Why do most telemedicine projects die after the research phase? We interviewed the companies that didn't — and found that personal relationships matter more than clinical evidence.

"Telemedicine companies with the ability to establish strong personal connections could be favoured over companies which provide strong clinical and economic evidence."
Korsgaard, Hasenkam & Vesterby — Health Services Management Research, 2021
The Challenge

Despite proven clinical validity, telemedicine consistently fails to transition from research projects into daily clinical practice. Providers are funded to build and test, but once the project phase ends, a sustainable business model is rarely in place.

My Role

Lead researcher and first author. Study design, case identification, structured interviews, Business Model Canvas analysis, and peer-reviewed publication.

Outcome

Published in Health Services Management Research (SAGE, 2021). The study identified personal relationships between company representatives and healthcare providers as the single most important factor in sustainable telemedicine implementation.

Finding the survivors

We started from all 418 Danish telemedicine initiatives registered in 2017, narrowing the field to home telemonitoring services that had been in continuous clinical operation — not project funding — for the longest period. Seven companies met the criteria, with operational track records ranging from three to eight years.

Each company was interviewed using a structured guide built around Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas, covering nine elements: customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partners, and cost structure. Interviews ran 90 to 120 minutes and were analysed using thematic coding.

It's about relationships

The central finding was that personal relationships between company representatives and healthcare providers drove everything else. They secured access to clinical ambassadors and lead users, enabled co-creation of value propositions, facilitated user education and support, and established indirect routes to the decision makers who actually controlled purchasing.

Value propositions focused on healthcare providers rather than patients: better use of clinical resources, ease of use, and employee satisfaction. Revenue came primarily from licensing. Strikingly, clinical and economic evidence — despite most companies having peer-reviewed documentation — was rarely cited as a reason for adoption. Relationships mattered more than evidence.

What the model doesn't solve

The research also identified structural blind spots in these business models. Patients, despite being the primary customer segment, were almost entirely absent from value propositions, channels, and customer relationships. Their needs were interpreted indirectly, through clinicians acting as 'patient experts'. None of the companies reported using patient data or automated analysis to improve their products.

A business model built on personal relationships is hard to scale. And in a publicly funded healthcare system, the person paying for the product is rarely the person using it — making it genuinely difficult to identify who needs to be convinced, and on what terms.
